\section{Introduction}
% =========================================================

Emotion analysis in natural language processing enables systems to capture nuanced emotional reactions in text. Traditional sentiment analysis typically focuses on polarity classification (positive, negative, neutral), but this is often insufficient to reflect the richness of human emotional expression. Aspect-based sentiment analysis (ABSA) addresses opinions directed at specific aspects of a text (e.g., food, service, or price in restaurant reviews). However, most ABSA research still relies solely on polarity, which obscures distinctions among emotional reactions such as \emph{admiration}, \emph{satisfaction}, \emph{gratitude}, \emph{annoyance}, and \emph{disappointment}.

In this work, we extend ABSA by incorporating aspect-level emotion annotations, thereby adding an emotion layer to the MAMS-ABSA dataset. This approach allows for modeling both aspect-specific evaluations and the emotional content of reviews.

% =========================================================
\section{Related Work}
% =========================================================

Emotion analysis in NLP has grown rapidly in recent years, yet the field remains fragmented. A large-scale survey by \citet{plazadelarco2024} shows that research activity increased significantly after 2018, but shared standards and consistent emotion frameworks are still lacking. Many studies rely by default on widely known models such as Ekman's basic emotions or Plutchik's Wheel, even when these frameworks may not suit the specific task or domain.

In addition to theoretical emotion models, commonly used datasets also reflect these design choices. A prominent example is the GoEmotions dataset \citep{demszky2020}, which is widely used for fine-grained emotion classification. It contains approximately 58,000 Reddit comments annotated across 27 emotions plus \emph{Neutral}, demonstrating that emotional expression in text goes far beyond simple polarity. However, GoEmotions was designed as a broad benchmark, and its label set is not necessarily optimal for more focused tasks such as aspect-level analysis within a specific domain.

Domain dependence plays a crucial role in emotion modelling because the range and intensity of emotions expressed can vary significantly across domains. In such settings, aspect-based analysis becomes essential, as opinions are typically directed not at an entity as a whole but at specific components or targets within it. A particularly relevant development is MAMS \citep{jiang2019}, which addresses a limitation of earlier ABSA datasets by including only sentences with multiple aspects expressing different polarities. This design prevents the task from collapsing into sentence-level sentiment classification.

Foundational benchmarks such as SemEval-2014 Task 4 \citep{pontiki2014} framed aspect-level opinion in terms of positive, negative, neutral, and conflict labels. Subsequent modelling work improved performance through target-dependent LSTMs \citep{tang2016}, attention mechanisms \citep{wang2016}, and transformer-based approaches such as BERT post-training \citep{xu2019}. However, polarity alone can obscure important distinctions: two positive statements may both indicate satisfaction, yet one may express admiration and another gratitude. Similarly, negative polarity may reflect disappointment, frustration, or disgust---each carrying different implications.

Overall, existing research reveals a gap between fine-grained emotion analysis and ABSA. Our work brings these strands together by extending a domain-specific ABSA benchmark with emotion-aware annotations.

% =========================================================
\section{Dataset \& Annotation Pipeline}
% =========================================================

Understanding emotions at the aspect level requires datasets where emotions are explicitly tied to individual aspects of a review. We addressed this gap by enriching an existing aspect-polarity dataset with aspect-specific emotion annotations. Given that manual annotation is time-consuming and labor-intensive, we employed a structured pipeline that combines initial manual labeling with LLM-assisted scaling, ensuring both accuracy and efficiency.

% ---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Source Dataset}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

For this study, we focus on restaurant reviews as the target domain, as they frequently contain multiple, clearly separable aspect evaluations within a single review. The Aspect Category Sentiment Analysis (ACSA) version of the MAMS dataset was chosen for its well-structured reviews, clearly defined aspects, and associated polarity labels. The aspect categories correspond to recurring components of restaurant reviews: \textbf{food}, \textbf{service}, \textbf{price}, \textbf{ambience}, \textbf{place}, \textbf{staff}, \textbf{menu}, and \textbf{miscellaneous}. Each aspect is assigned one of three polarity labels: positive, negative, or neutral. The dataset is split into three standard partitions: 7,090 training instances, 888 validation instances, and 901 test instances.

% ---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Emotion Framework Design}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

The design of the emotion framework was guided by the limitations of existing models when applied to ABSA in the restaurant review domain. Large-scale emotion frameworks such as GoEmotions (28 categories) introduce ambiguity in short review texts, where overlapping or subtle distinctions are difficult to identify consistently. Moreover, many categories are not relevant to the restaurant domain---for example, \emph{grief} rarely occurs in dining reviews.

The selection of the final emotion set was carried out through an iterative and collaborative process. As an initial step, the GoEmotions label set was applied to the MAMS dataset to explore which emotions were most commonly expressed. Plutchik's Wheel of Emotions was used as a theoretical reference for understanding intensity and conceptual similarity. Based on empirical observations and theoretical insights, we compiled an initial list of approximately 20 candidate emotions. Six annotators then conducted annotations at the aspect level, and after each round, disagreements were reviewed and the label set was revised through structured discussion and voting.

This led to a final set of \textbf{nine emotion categories}, selected to capture clear and meaningful customer reactions while remaining distinguishable in practice:

\subsection{Inter-Annotator Agreement}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

A subset of 50 reviews was selected for IAA evaluation. All annotators independently assigned one of the nine predefined emotions to each aspect. Inter-Annotator Agreement was measured using \textbf{Krippendorff's Alpha} (nominal), which accounts for chance agreement and supports multiple annotators:

\begin{equation}
\alpha = 1 - \frac{D_o}{D_e}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $D_o$ is the observed disagreement and $D_e$ is the expected disagreement. For nominal labels, disagreement is 0 if labels are identical and 1 otherwise.

\begin{keybox}{IAA Result}
The resulting \textbf{Krippendorff's $\alpha = 0.7196$} indicates \emph{substantial agreement} among annotators, suggesting that the annotation guidelines effectively supported consistent labeling despite the inherent subjectivity of emotion annotation.
\end{keybox}

\section{Methodology and Experimental Setup}
% =========================================================

% ---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Experimental Design}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

Our experimental framework evaluates models across two complementary tasks:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Fine-grained emotion prediction:} Models classify each aspect--text pair into one of nine emotion categories. Input consists solely of review text and associated aspect(s), without access to polarity labels during inference.
  \item \textbf{Coarse-grained polarity prediction:} Models predict three polarity classes (positive, negative, neutral). Evaluated under two conditions: \emph{single-task} (polarity only) and \emph{multi-task} (jointly trained on emotion and polarity objectives).
\end{itemize}

The purpose of the multi-task comparison is to determine whether fine-grained emotion supervision enriches learned representations and consequently improves polarity prediction performance.

% ---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Data Representation and Model Architectures}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

Each instance consists of a review sentence paired with an aspect category, a polarity label (3 classes), and an emotion label (9 classes). All inputs are constructed at the aspect level using the format:

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=lightgray, colframe=medgray, arc=3pt]
\texttt{x = "TEXT: \{review\} | ASPECT: \{aspect\}"}
\end{tcolorbox}

For emotion prediction, seven transformer architectures are evaluated:
\textbf{BERT-base}, \textbf{BERT-large}, \textbf{DistilBERT}, \textbf{RoBERTa-base}, \textbf{RoBERTa-large}, \textbf{DistilRoBERTa}, and \textbf{DeBERTa-base}. For polarity prediction, \textbf{DistilRoBERTa}, \textbf{SVM}, and \textbf{Logistic Regression} were also evaluated.

For multi-task learning, a shared transformer backbone is combined with task-specific classification heads for 9-class emotion and 3-class polarity prediction.

% ---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Training Configuration and Evaluation}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

All transformer models are trained using the Hugging Face Trainer framework with deterministic data loading. Early stopping is employed with a patience of three epochs based on validation loss. Balanced class weights are incorporated into the loss function to prevent bias toward majority classes.

Model performance is evaluated using three complementary metrics: \textbf{Accuracy}, \textbf{Weighted F1} (accounts for class imbalance), and \textbf{Macro F1} (unweighted average, emphasizing minority class performance).

% =========================================================
\section{Discussion}
% =========================================================

% ---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Model Behavior and Context Sensitivity}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

The results indicate that emotion classification in ABSA is strongly context-dependent. Emotion classification is aspect-dependent: the same sentence may express different emotions toward different aspects. TF-IDF-based representations collapse these distinctions, whereas transformer encoders produce contextualized, aspect-conditioned embeddings. Interestingly, model performance does not scale directly with model size---base and distilled models achieve results comparable to or better than larger architectures.

% ---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Impact of Class Imbalance}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

The dataset exhibits substantial class imbalance, with frequent labels such as \texttt{mentioned\_only} dominating the distribution, while others (e.g., \emph{disgust}, \emph{gratitude}, \emph{mixed\_emotions}) occur only rarely. This imbalance reflects patterns in real-world review data, making it an inherent challenge for emotion-aware ABSA rather than a problem that can be fully eliminated through dataset curation.

The prominence of \texttt{mentioned\_only} reflects a meaningful design decision: in aspect-level analysis, many aspect mentions do not carry an emotional signal. Including this category helps focus the analysis on meaningful emotional expressions, improving label clarity and reducing noise in the learning signal.

% ---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Role of Emotion in Representation Learning}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

The multi-task experiments show that incorporating emotion supervision consistently improves polarity prediction across all models, even though emotion labels are not available at inference time. Although the improvements are moderate, they are consistent across architectures, suggesting that fine-grained emotion information provides complementary signals beyond coarse polarity labels.

% ---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Practical Implications for Emotion-Aware ABSA}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

The results suggest that emotion-aware ABSA is feasible but remains constrained by data availability. The combination of substantial inter-annotator agreement and consistent modeling results indicates that the proposed 9-label emotion framework is usable in practice. It captures meaningful emotional distinctions while remaining clear and easy for annotators to apply consistently.

% ---------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Benchmark Utility}
% ---------------------------------------------------------

The resulting dataset provides a structured resource for aspect-level sentiment and emotion modeling. By combining aspect, polarity, and fine-grained emotion annotations, it addresses limitations of existing resources that are restricted to either coarse polarity or non-aspect-aware emotion labels. Human annotations show substantial agreement (Krippendorff's $\alpha = 0.72$), and LLM-assisted scaling enables reasonable coverage for model training.

% =========================================================
\section{Limitations}
% =========================================================

Several limitations should be acknowledged:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=4pt]
  \item The proposed emotion framework is \textbf{domain-specific} and was developed for restaurant reviews, limiting its immediate generalizability to other domains.
  \item The majority of instances were labeled using \textbf{LLM-assisted annotation}, which may introduce systematic annotation noise or model-induced bias.
  \item The dataset exhibits \textbf{substantial class imbalance}, with several emotion categories containing very few instances, making reliable modeling of minority classes difficult.
  \item The current framework \textbf{assumes aspect terms are already extracted}, limiting applicability in fully end-to-end real-world systems.
\end{enumerate}

% =========================================================
\section{Future Work}
% =========================================================

Future research should evaluate the proposed emotion framework beyond the restaurant domain to assess its generalizability. Expanding the manually annotated portion of the dataset would further validate the labeling scheme and reduce reliance on LLM-assisted annotations. Additionally, more expressive formulations such as multi-label or intensity-based emotion modeling could better capture the complexity of affective states. Addressing class imbalance through improved sampling or cost-sensitive learning strategies may enhance minority-class performance. Finally, future work may explore joint end-to-end models that integrate aspect extraction, polarity prediction, and emotion classification.

% =========================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% =========================================================

We presented an emotion-aware extension of the MAMS-ABSA dataset, combining aspect-level annotations with both polarity and nine fine-grained emotion categories. Our human-annotated benchmark shows substantial inter-annotator agreement (Krippendorff's $\alpha = 0.72$), demonstrating that the emotion framework is understandable and reasonably consistent for manual labeling.

Experimental results indicate that transformer-based models can learn meaningful aspect-emotion distinctions, although performance is limited for rare classes due to natural class imbalance. Multi-task learning further shows that emotion supervision can improve polarity prediction, confirming that fine-grained emotion information complements traditional sentiment labels.

Overall, the dataset provides a structured and reliable resource for aspect-level sentiment and emotion modeling. By bridging the gap between coarse polarity labels and non-aspect-aware emotion datasets, it offers a foundation for future research in emotion-aware ABSA and supports the evaluation of modern NLP models on tasks that require both nuance and aspect specificity.
